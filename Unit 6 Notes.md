## 6.1 – Proofs

### Proof
A logical argument that uses **deductive reasoning** to show the truth of a statement.

### Two-Column Proof
A series of numbered statements and corresponding reasons that show an argument in logical order.

**Example** – Vertical Angles Congruence Theorem:
- **Given:** ∠1 and ∠3 are vertical angles
- **Prove:** ∠1 ≅ ∠3

| Statement | Reason |
|---|---|
| ∠1 and ∠3 are vertical angles | Given |
| ∠1 & ∠2 are a linear pair; ∠2 & ∠3 are a linear pair | Definition of Linear Pair |
| ∠1 & ∠2 are supplementary; ∠2 & ∠3 are supplementary | Linear Pair Postulate |
| ∠1 ≅ ∠3 | Congruent Supplements Theorem |

### Paragraph Proof
A written proof that uses words to outline a logical flow to an argument.

**Example** – Given AB ∥ CD, Prove ∠1 ≅ ∠2:
Since AB is parallel to CD and AC is a transversal of AB and CD, it holds that angles on both transversed lines will be congruent. Since angle 1 and angle 2 are in the positions respective to their parallel lines, it holds that ∠1 ≅ ∠2.

## 6.2 – Perpendicular Bisectors & Angle Bisectors

### Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Perpendicular (⊥)** | When two lines intersect at a right angle (also called Orthogonal) |
| **Bisector** | An object that splits another object into two equal pieces |
| **Equidistant** | Two objects that are the same distance from a common point |

### Perpendicular Bisector Theorem
If a point (C) lies on the perpendicular bisector (CP ⊥ bisects AB) of a segment, then it is equidistant from the endpoints (A) and (B) of the segment.
> AC ≅ CB

### Converse of the Perpendicular Bisector Theorem
If a point (D) is equidistant from the endpoints of a segment (AB), then the point lies on the perpendicular bisector (CP) of the segment.

### Angle Bisector Theorem
If a point (D) lies on the bisector of an angle (AD bisects ∠BAC), then the point is equidistant perpendicularly to the legs of the angle (AB) and (AC).
> AC ⊥ CD and AB ⊥ BP
> CD ≅ BD

### Converse of the Angle Bisector Theorem
If a point (D) is in the interior of an angle (∠BAC) and the segments perpendicular to the legs of the angle that intersect at that point (BD) and (CD) are congruent, then that point lies on the bisector (AD) of the angle.

## 6.3 – Triangle Bisectors (Circumcenter & Incenter)

### Concurrent
When 3 or more lines, rays, or segments intersect at the same point (point of concurrency).

### Circumcenter
The point of concurrency of the **perpendicular bisectors** of the legs of a triangle.

#### Circumcenter Theorem
The circumcenter of a triangle is **equidistant from the vertices** of the triangle.

#### Circumcenter Locations
| Triangle Type | Circumcenter Location |
|---|---|
| Acute triangle | Inside the triangle |
| Right triangle | On the triangle (at the hypotenuse midpoint) |
| Obtuse triangle | Outside the triangle |

#### Circumscribed Circle
Circumscribed triangles share their circumcenter with the center of the circle.

### Incenter
The point where the **angle bisectors** of a triangle are concurrent. The incenter **always lies within** the triangle.

#### Incenter Theorem
The incenter of a triangle is **equidistant from the sides** of the triangle.

#### Inscribed Circle
A circle within a triangle whose center is the same as the incenter of the triangle.

## 6.4 – Medians and Altitudes of Triangles (Centroid & Orthocenter)

### Median of a Triangle
A segment from a **vertex** of a triangle to the **midpoint of the opposing side**.

### Centroid
The point of concurrency of all **three medians** of a triangle.

#### Centroid Theorem
The centroid of a triangle is **two-thirds** of the distance from each vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side.

> If SQ = 8, find QW:
> - SQ = (2/3)SW → 8 = (2/3)SW → SW = 12
> - SW = SQ + QW → 12 = 8 + QW → **QW = 4**

### Altitude of a Triangle
The **perpendicular segment** from a vertex to the opposite side, or the line that contains the opposite side.

### Orthocenter
The point of concurrency of the **altitudes** of a triangle.

#### Orthocenter Locations
| Triangle Type | Orthocenter Location |
|---|---|
| Acute triangle | Inside the triangle |
| Right triangle | On the triangle (at the vertex of the right angle) |
| Obtuse triangle | Outside the triangle |

### Concept Summary – Segments, Lines, Rays, and Points in Triangles

| Segment Type | Point of Concurrency | Property |
|---|---|---|
| Perpendicular bisector | **Circumcenter** | Equidistant from the *vertices* of the triangle |
| Angle bisector | **Incenter** | Equidistant from the *sides* of the triangle |
| Median | **Centroid** | Two-thirds of the distance from each vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side |
| Altitude | **Orthocenter** | Lines containing the altitudes are concurrent at the orthocenter |

## 6.5 – Triangle Midsegment Theorem

### Midsegment (Midline) of a Triangle
A segment joining the **midpoints of two sides** of a triangle.

### Triangle Midsegment Theorem
The midsegment of a triangle, which joins the midpoints of two sides of a triangle, is:
1. **Parallel** to the third side of the triangle
2. **Half the length** of that third side

> DE is a mid-segment; D is midpoint of AC; E is midpoint of CB
> - DE ∥ AB
> - DE = ½ · AB

**Examples:**
1. Given M, N midpoints and MN = 12 → find DF: **DF = 24**
2. Given D, E midpoints, DE = 3x − 5, AB = 26 → find x:
   - 3x − 5 = ½(26) = 13 → 3x = 18 → **x = 6**
3. Given right △RST, G/N/J midpoints, ST = 6, RS = 8 → find perimeter of △GNJ:
   - Each side of △GNJ = half the opposite side → sides = 4, 3, and ½RT
   - RT = √(6² + 8²) = √100 = 10 → half = 5 → perimeter = **4 + 3 + 5 = 12**

## Quick Reference – Special Points in a Triangle

| Point | Formed By | Key Property | Always Inside? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Circumcenter** | Perpendicular bisectors | Equidistant from all 3 vertices | No (outside for obtuse) |
| **Incenter** | Angle bisectors | Equidistant from all 3 sides | **Yes** |
| **Centroid** | Medians | 2/3 from vertex to opposite midpoint | **Yes** |
| **Orthocenter** | Altitudes | — | No (outside for obtuse) |
